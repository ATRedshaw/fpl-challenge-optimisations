Finding the optimal lineup
for every FPL Challenge.

A machine learning model generates per-player expected-point forecasts, which a linear programme then uses to find the mathematically optimal squad for each FPL Challenge constraint set. The pre-gameweek selection is compared against the hindsight optimal to measure how well the model performed. Spoiler: it doesn't always cover itself in glory.

How it works

The approach and reading the results

The pipeline

Three stages run in sequence to produce a predicted optimal lineup before every challenge.

Stage 1
ML Forecasting
A private model produces per-player xPts forecasts from match context and historical performance features.
Stage 2
Rule Adjustment
Raw xPts are adjusted for each challenge's scoring rules, from simple multipliers through to Poisson-modelled event probabilities and probabilistic win weights.
Stage 3
LP Optimisation
An integer linear programme selects the squad that maximises adjusted xPts within the challenge's constraints: e.g. budget, position limits, multipliers and club caps.

The hindsight benchmark

Once a gameweek's scores are confirmed, the same LP solver runs again using actual points rather than forecasts. The result is the true with-hindsight optimal: the best possible lineup anyone could have picked with perfect foreknowledge. Which, to be fair, is cheating.

It is an unreachable ceiling by design. No pre-gameweek prediction can match it, but it gives an honest upper bound to measure against. Players appearing in both lineups are highlighted in green inside each challenge view.

What you see on the challenge cards

Each completed challenge card gives a quick summary of how the prediction performed. Here is what each element means.

  • xPts → Optimal pts. The two numbers separated by an arrow are the model's forecast point total for the predicted lineup (xPts) and the actual points that the hindsight-optimal lineup scored. The arrow is not a judgement. It is just an arrow.
  • Prediction ratio. The percentage shown alongside those two figures is the prediction ratio: the model's xPts forecast expressed as a share of the hindsight ceiling. A higher figure means the model's forecast was closer to the best achievable result.
  • Match rate. The colour-coded percentage and progress bar below show how many players in the predicted lineup also appeared in the hindsight-optimal squad. This is discussed in more detail further down.
  • Scored pts and GW rank. When a personal result has been recorded for a challenge, the card also shows the actual points scored and the gameweek rank achieved with that submission.

Inside the challenge view

Opening a completed challenge reveals both lineups side by side along with a detailed breakdown of the comparison. The totals panel at the top shows three figures: the personal submission score and gameweek rank, the model's xPts forecast for the predicted squad and the hindsight-optimal lineup's actual score with its budget usage.

The comparison panel at the bottom is where the meaningful analysis lives. It contains the following statistics.

  • Match rate. The number of players the predicted lineup shares with the hindsight optimal, expressed as a percentage of the total squad size.
  • Hindsight gap. The raw points difference between the hindsight-optimal score and the model's xPts forecast. This tells you how far the ceiling was above what the model expected to achieve.
  • Prediction ratio. The model's xPts forecast as a percentage of the hindsight-optimal score. The closer to 100%, the better the model judged the points available in that particular challenge.

When a personal result is available, a second block of figures appears beneath the prediction analysis.

  • GW rank. Where the actual submission finished relative to all other participants in that specific gameweek's challenge.
  • Overall rank. The season-long cumulative rank at the time of that gameweek, shown alongside a percentile for context. Raw rank numbers are easier to interpret when you know what fraction of the field they represent.
  • Pts vs optimal. The submission's actual score minus the hindsight ceiling. This will almost always be negative. A positive figure would mean the submission outscored the theoretically perfect lineup, which would be quite the achievement.
  • Pts vs predicted. The submission's actual score minus the model's xPts forecast. Positive means the lineup outperformed what the model expected. The model quietly keeps track.

Reading the match rate

Match rate is the share of players the predicted lineup has in common with the hindsight-optimal. The number will look low, and that is expected. Each challenge has its own squad format and size, but the eligible player pool is always around 600. The number of valid squad combinations is astronomically large regardless of how many players you are picking. Getting any meaningful fraction of the optimal selection right before a ball has been kicked is a genuinely good result.

A 50% match rate means half the predicted squad matched the best possible selection chosen from roughly 600 players. The probability of landing that by chance is effectively zero. The model is finding real signal.

A quick illustration. Take a simplified version: a 7-player squad built from pools of just 50 players per position, with one goalkeeper and between one and three players from each outfield position. The number of valid combinations already comes to roughly 452 billion. Real FPL Challenge has around 600 players in total, squad sizes that change every week, captaincy to assign and budget limits on top. The true search space is often orders of magnitude larger.

Granted, a fair few of those 452 billion options involve three suspended players and someone who last started a match in October. Realistic picks narrow things down considerably, but the scale illustrates why landing a meaningful share of the optimal squad, without the benefit of hindsight, is harder than it sounds.

The prediction ratio matters more

Player overlap only tells part of the story. Two completely different squads can score nearly identical points when the market has priced in the same likely contributors. The more telling measure is how close the model's xPts forecast comes to the hindsight ceiling, expressed here as the prediction ratio.

The prediction ratio is the model's xPts forecast as a percentage of the hindsight-optimal score. A figure of 100% would mean the model forecast exactly as many points as the best possible lineup actually scored. That will never happen in practice, and unlike match rate, there is no comfortable statistical argument for why a lower figure is still impressive. The hindsight optimal is the ceiling, and the ratio tells you how close the model got.

A prediction ratio in the high sixties is a strong result. It means the model forecast roughly two-thirds of the maximum points available, chosen before a single match was played and with no knowledge of injuries, rotations or who would have an unexpectedly brilliant afternoon.

The season standing

The standing bar beneath the stats strip shows the current overall rank across all challenges played so far this season. It updates after each gameweek as results are recorded. Clicking it opens a chart of how rank has progressed gameweek by gameweek.

The chart panel also shows the season-high rank, the percentile at the current standing and the total rank movement since the first challenge. Whether that movement is upwards or downwards is, diplomatically, left as an exercise for the reader.

The overall rank is cumulative across the entire season rather than a snapshot from a single gameweek, so it tends to be a more stable and meaningful reflection of consistent performance than any individual challenge result.
